The one-on-one is the manager's actual product — not the roadmap deck, not the sprint review, not the all-hands slide.

I learned this the hard way leading through a migration quarter: two seniors left within six weeks, and exit interviews did not mention salary. They mentioned skipped weeks, status-only check-ins, and the feeling that nobody was tracking whether the work still made sense for them. The pipeline of people was leaking while I was optimizing the pipeline of features.

They treat one-on-ones as overhead because the output is invisible until someone leaves. That is the measurement failure, not the meeting. Thirty focused minutes — notes taken, follow-ups closed, career questions answered honestly — is how you detect drift before it becomes attrition.

Protect the slot before you protect the standup. Cancel the sync that could have been async. The team you keep is downstream of the conversations you actually had.

— JV · Dark Heart Labs.
