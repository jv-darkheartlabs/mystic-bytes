Every collection is a curatorial act — each inclusion and omission is an argument about what matters.

I rebuilt the mystic-bytes readings shelf and found the theory encoded in the tags: three overlapping "enemies-to-lovers" variants, a pile of one-off mood labels, and nothing connecting captor tropes to the browse path users actually take. The About page said "romantasy curation." The folder structure said "whatever imported last." Browse logs showed readers landing on trope pages we never linked from the main shelf — proof the manifesto and the navigation disagreed.[^1]

Prune on purpose. Merge synonyms. Delete what you keep avoiding. Reorder by the question a reader asks, not the order you imported. Notice what you keep adding and what you keep skipping. Other people's collections reveal them faster than their bios because collections are unguarded — no copywriter, just repeated choices.

Your collection is a delayed mirror. Curate it like you mean to be seen.

— JV · Dark Heart Labs.

[^1]: Susan Pearce, *Interpreting Objects and Collections* (Routledge, 1994) — on how assembled objects encode collector intent and social meaning beyond individual items.
