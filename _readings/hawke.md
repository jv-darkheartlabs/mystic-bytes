## Hook & thesis
In *Hawke*, Jescie Hall masterfully crafts a narrative where devotion is as chilling as it is intoxicating, revealing the fine line between love and madness. **Verdict:** This book is a harrowing exploration of obsession that grips the reader with its dark allure and relentless tension.

## The contextual pivot
Positioned firmly within the stalker-obsession subgenre, *Hawke* juxtaposes the thrill of dark romance against the unsettling backdrop of invasive desire. Hall's prose recalls the atmospheric intensity of classics like *Rebecca* by Daphne du Maurier, while also tapping into contemporary narratives that challenge the boundaries of morality and sanity, such as *You* by Caroline Kepnes. The invisible audience includes avid readers of psychological thrillers and dark romance enthusiasts who revel in the complexities of flawed characters and the blurred lines of affection. Hall's work bridges niche tropes to appeal to general readers, ensuring a visceral experience that lingers long after the last page.

## Deep-dive critique
Structurally, *Hawke* employs a non-linear timeline that amplifies the suspense as it unfolds the protagonist’s spiraling descent into obsession. The dual perspectives—one from the stalker and one from the victim—create a disorienting yet compelling narrative that challenges the reader's sympathies. Hall’s prose is sharp and evocative, with dialogue that crackles with tension, reflecting the characters’ internal turmoil and simmering desires.

The pacing fluctuates between moments of breathless anticipation and eerie stillness, effectively mirroring the emotional landscape of its characters. However, there are moments where the pacing falters, particularly in the middle sections where the intensity wanes, requiring a more focused tightening of the narrative threads. The thematic exploration of devotion as a double-edged sword is both poignant and unsettling, revealing how love can morph into a form of possession, a concept expertly woven into the characters’ interactions.

The ending, though climactic, leaves some threads intriguingly unresolved, inviting further contemplation on the nature of obsession and its consequences. This ambiguity is both a strength and a potential frustration, as it challenges the conventional expectations of resolution in the romance genre.

## Discussion launchpad
*Hawke* ignites polarizing discussions about the ethics of desire and the portrayal of obsession in relationships. The moral ambiguity of the characters invites readers to grapple with their own biases regarding love and control. What remains unsaid in Hall's narrative is the societal implications of glorifying such obsessive love, which could spark heated debate.

**Questions for the room:**  
- How do we balance the romanticization of obsession with its darker implications?  
- Does the dual perspective enhance or detract from the overall experience of the story?  
- What are the ethical boundaries in portrayals of love that turn toxic?  
- How does Hall’s use of pacing influence your emotional response to the characters?  
- In what ways does the ending challenge traditional romance tropes?
