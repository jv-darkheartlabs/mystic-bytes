## Hook & thesis
In *I Will Mend You*, Gigi Styx crafts a harrowing exploration of obsession and the human psyche under duress, where caretaking becomes a twisted art form. **Verdict:** This book is a dark and twisted ode to desire, revealing the fine line between love and control.

## The contextual pivot
Treading the shadowy corridors of the stalker-obsession subgenre, Styx's narrative intertwines elements of gothic romance with a contemporary pulse. It stands among works like Anna Zaires's *Twist Me* and Tessa Bailey’s explorations of possessive love, yet creates a unique footprint with its medical horror overtones. The invisible audience leans toward readers who revel in the darker aspects of love, where intimacy morphs into something sinister and suffocating, inviting both niche enthusiasts and curious newcomers to engage with its unsettling charm.

## Deep-dive critique
The structure of *I Will Mend You* operates like a diary, drawing readers into the protagonist’s labyrinthine thoughts, which heightens the tension and disorientation. Styx’s decision to use a first-person narrative plunges us into a suffocating intimacy, allowing us to feel the protagonist’s obsession and her skewed perceptions. The pacing is deliberate, with moments of languor that mirror the protagonist's obsessive fixation, creating a sense of dread that crescendos effectively without rushing to resolution. 

Characterization excels, particularly in the portrayal of the protagonist’s descent into obsession, which is both compelling and horrifying. Dialogues are fraught with an unsettling tension, reflecting the duality of care and control. However, the narrative occasionally falters in world-building; the surrounding characters remain somewhat two-dimensional, serving primarily as foils rather than fully realized individuals. The ending, while shocking, raises questions about the nature of healing and whether true redemption is ever achievable after such profound trauma, leaving the reader in a state of disquiet.

## Discussion launchpad
Styx’s work undeniably invites discomfort, particularly in its exploration of the fine line between love and possession. The moral ambiguity of the protagonist’s actions raises critical questions about agency and desire in relationships marked by obsession.  
**Questions for the room:**  
- How does Styx's portrayal of obsession challenge traditional romantic tropes?  
- In what ways does the diary format enhance or detract from the reader's connection to the protagonist?  
- Can a character engaging in such morally ambiguous behavior ever be deemed sympathetic?  
- What does this narrative suggest about the nature of healing and the potential for redemption?  
- How do we reconcile the fine line between passion and control within our own readings of romance?
