## Hook & thesis
*Kingpin* by Eden O'Neill delivers a visceral exploration of power dynamics and desire within the hallowed halls of Court University. **Verdict:** This novel masterfully intertwines the intoxicating allure of dominance with the perilous nature of vulnerability, creating a dark romance that grips the reader's psyche.

## The contextual pivot
Occupying a niche within the bully-romance subgenre, *Kingpin* stands as a compelling sequel in O'Neill's Court University series. Readers familiar with the genre will find echoes of themes present in Tiffany Reisz's *The Original Sinners* series and Penelope Douglas's *Fall Away* series, where power and emotional manipulation reign supreme. O'Neill deftly bridges the divide between niche tropes and broader audiences by embedding rich character studies within the thrilling machinations of university life. The invisible audience here is the dark romance aficionado seeking depth amidst the tumult of adolescent power plays.

## Deep-dive critique
O'Neill employs a tight third-person limited perspective that allows for a deep dive into the psyche of her characters, particularly the enigmatic protagonist. The pacing oscillates between tension-filled moments and reflective passages, creating a rhythm that echoes the characters' emotional turmoil. The dialogue sparkles with an electric charge, showcasing O'Neill's knack for pushing boundaries while maintaining a tense atmosphere. 

However, the novel's structure occasionally falters; certain plot threads feel rushed or underexplored, potentially leaving some readers yearning for more profound connections. This is particularly evident in minor character arcs that could have enriched the narrative fabric. The thematic exploration of power, identity, and the blurred lines of affection is compelling, yet the ending, while climactic, risks feeling somewhat conventional for seasoned readers of the genre. Ultimately, the emotional crescendos resonate, leaving an aftertaste of both satisfaction and yearning.

## Discussion launchpad
The book's exploration of power dynamics invites reflection on its darker implications and the nature of consent within relationships framed by manipulation and rivalry. Where does the line blur between attraction and obsession, and how does this shape our understanding of love in a toxic environment? 

**Questions for the room:**  
- How effectively does O'Neill challenge traditional notions of consent in her portrayal of power imbalance?  
- In what ways do the character arcs reflect or subvert conventional villain archetypes in romance?  
- Does the pacing of the story enhance or detract from the emotional stakes presented?  
- How does the setting of Court University function as a character in its own right, shaping the narrative's tension?  
- What are the implications of the novel's resolution for the characters' future dynamics?
