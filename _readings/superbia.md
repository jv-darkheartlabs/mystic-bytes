## Hook & thesis
In *Superbia*, Colette Rhodes weaves a dark tapestry where pride manifests as both a sin and an intoxicating allure, captivating the reader’s senses. **Verdict:** This book excels in its exploration of the seductive dance between power and vulnerability, leaving a haunting echo long after the last page is turned.

## The contextual pivot
*Superbia* sits comfortably within the fantasy-romance genre, yet it diverges into a realm that feels both familiar and strikingly unique. Rhodes’s work can be juxtaposed with authors like Sarah J. Maas and Jennifer L. Armentrout, where the threads of desire intertwine with the supernatural, yet Rhodes’s prose embraces a darker, more gothic sensibility. The invisible audience here is likely those who revel in morally ambiguous characters and complex emotional landscapes, bridging niche tropes with a wider appeal that invites general readers into the abyss of human desire and sin.

## Deep-dive critique
The narrative structure of *Superbia* is layered, unfolding through a dual perspective that heightens the tension between the protagonists' inner turmoil and their external conflicts. This choice of dual POV, while enriching the narrative, occasionally leads to pacing issues, particularly in the middle sections where the lush description of their surroundings can distract from the core emotional stakes. The dialogue, often laced with subtext, serves to deepen the characters' relationships and the overarching themes of pride and possession. The world-building is exquisite—Rhodes paints a vivid landscape of seduction and danger that feels alive, wrapping the reader in an almost palpable atmosphere of yearning.

However, the ending veers into familiar territory, which, while satisfying for many, may not fully reward the intricate build-up established earlier. This could be seen as a flaw, leaving some readers craving a more daring resolution that matches the intensity of earlier revelations. Themes of pride and the consequences it invites resonate powerfully, as the characters grapple with their desires, ultimately revealing the cost of their choices.

## Discussion launchpad
*Superbia* presents a rich ground for discussion, particularly regarding the dual nature of desire and the portrayal of pride as both an empowering and destructive force. The tension between character agency and predestination offers fertile ground for debate about free will in romantic entanglements.

- In what ways does Rhodes challenge traditional notions of pride in romance?  
- Do you find the ending satisfactory or a missed opportunity for deeper thematic exploration?  
- How do the elements of dark fantasy enhance or detract from the romantic tension?  
- What moral ambiguities in the characters resonated with you, and why?
