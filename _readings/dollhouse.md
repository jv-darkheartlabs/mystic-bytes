## Hook & thesis
*Dollhouse* by Kyla Faye is a haunting exploration of identity and possession, where love becomes an intricate dance within the confines of a claustrophobic world. **Verdict:** Faye masterfully intertwines the grotesque and the romantic, crafting a narrative that lingers long after the final page.

## The contextual pivot
This gothic-horror-romance firmly situates itself in the contemporary lineage of dark romance, echoing the atmospheric dread of authors like Anna-Marie McLemore and the psychological complexity of Tarryn Fisher. Faye's work transcends typical genre boundaries, appealing to both niche enthusiasts and general readers alike by weaving threads of horror and longing through a narrative tapestry that feels both intimate and expansive. The invisible audience is one that craves depth in emotional landscapes, where desire is often as perilous as it is intoxicating.

## Deep-dive critique
Faye’s structure plays a pivotal role in amplifying the novel's themes, with a non-linear timeline that mirrors the disjointed nature of the protagonist’s psyche. The dual perspective POV creates an unsettling intimacy, allowing readers to oscillate between yearning and dread. The pacing is deliberate, drawing out the tension — the moments of heat are juxtaposed with chilling revelations, sustaining a rhythm that keeps the reader ensnared in its web.

The prose is richly atmospheric, drenched in vivid descriptions that evoke the senses and elicit visceral reactions; Faye’s characters inhabit their spaces like the dolls they are compared to — beautifully crafted yet ultimately fragile. Dialogue serves not only as exposition but as a reflection of the characters’ internal conflicts, showcasing their struggles against the constraints of their own existence. However, the reliance on certain gothic tropes leaves some moments feeling predictable, and while the ending is impactful, it may feel abrupt for those seeking closure in traditional narrative arcs.

## Discussion launchpad
The tension between agency and captivity lies at the heart of *Dollhouse*, often leaving readers grappling with uncomfortable truths about love and possession. Beneath the allure of dark romance, Faye poses questions about the nature of selfhood in a world that seeks to objectify.

**Questions for the room:**  
- How does the dual POV shape your understanding of the characters?  
- In what ways does the setting amplify the themes of identity and possession?  
- Can the predictability of certain tropes enhance or detract from the overall impact?  
- How does Faye’s portrayal of desire challenge traditional romantic norms?  
- What unresolved conflicts linger for you after the ending?
