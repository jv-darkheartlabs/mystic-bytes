## Hook & thesis
*Carnival Stalker* by Selena Winters thrusts readers into a dizzying world where obsession intertwines with the mercurial energy of a carnival, creating a haunting exploration of desire and power. **Verdict:** This novel unearths the terrifying beauty of longing, leaving us to question the thin line between admiration and obsession.

## The contextual pivot
Nestled within the stalker-obsession subgenre of dark romance, *Carnival Stalker* presents a tapestry of vivid imagery and emotional turmoil typical of Winters' oeuvre. While her prior works have danced around themes of forbidden love and psychological tension, this novel intensifies those elements, creating a visceral experience. The carnival setting serves not only as a backdrop but as a living entity that amplifies the characters’ desires, drawing in an audience familiar with the dark allure of obsession while enticing those who may be new to such themes.

## Deep-dive critique
Winters employs a dual perspective that oscillates between the stalker and the pursued, allowing readers to immerse themselves in both the thrill and terror of their relationship. This choice of point of view creates a sense of claustrophobia, heightening the stakes and intensifying emotional engagement. The pacing, deliberate and seductive, mirrors the gradual unraveling of the characters’ psyches, with the carnival rides serving as metaphors for their spiraling descent into madness.

The prose is sharp and evocative, painting scenes that pulse with both color and dread. Dialogue crackles with tension, revealing the characters' inner turmoil while simultaneously drawing the reader deeper into their entwined fates. Yet, while the world-building is rich, some character motivations falter, leaning too heavily on archetypes that could have benefitted from deeper exploration. The climax, while thrilling, straddles the line between earned and excessive, raising questions about the sustainability of such heightened stakes.

## Discussion launchpad
The intricacies of desire and obsession are at the forefront of this narrative, prompting us to consider where the allure of darkness resides within us. What happens when admiration morphs into something sinister? 

**Questions for the room:**  
- How effectively does the dual perspective enhance or detract from the story's emotional weight?  
- In what ways does the carnival setting function as a character in its own right?  
- Do the characters’ motivations resonate as authentic, or do they risk becoming mere vessels for the plot?  
- How does the ending reflect or challenge the themes of power dynamics and possession?  
- What are the implications of portraying obsession as a form of love?
