## Hook & thesis
In A.S. Fenichel’s *A Lady’s Honor*, the author wrestles with the weight of societal expectations and personal desire, weaving a tale that critiques the very fabric of honor in the Regency era. **Verdict:** This novel is a compelling exploration of how love can subvert the strictures of reputation, leaving readers both intrigued and yearning for more.

## The contextual pivot
* A Lady's Honor* occupies a unique niche within the fantasy-romance genre, specifically within the realm of Regency literature, marrying historical context with elements of romantic fantasy. Fenichel’s work stands alongside authors like Sarah MacLean and Tessa Dare, yet it diverges by embedding its narrative within the mystical undercurrents of the Everton Domestic Society, a setting where reputation is as fickle as the characters’ desires. The book appeals to an audience that delights in the intersection of societal critique and romance, making it accessible to general readers while simultaneously catering to those familiar with its niche tropes.

## Deep-dive critique
Fenichel's structure effectively propels the story, alternating between the perspectives of characters caught in a web of honor and clandestine desire. The pacing, particularly in the first half, builds tension through a slow reveal of secrets, enhancing the reader's investment in the characters’ fates. The use of dual POVs provides insight into the internal conflicts that drive the narrative — a choice that deepens emotional resonance and emphasizes the stakes involved in each character's choices. 

Characterization is a notable strength; each figure within the Everton Society is crafted with distinct motivations, creating a vibrant tableau of ambition and longing. The dialogue sparkles with wit, often undercutting the rigid decorum expected of the time, which highlights the subversive nature of the protagonists’ desires. However, the mechanical flaws emerge in the latter half of the novel, where the pacing falters as the plot rushes to its conclusion, leaving some character arcs feeling underdeveloped. The ending, while dramatic, risks alienating readers who may feel it lacks sufficient buildup.

## Discussion launchpad
The novel invites debate over the effectiveness of its romantic resolutions versus the societal constraints it critiques. While the central theme of honor is compelling, some readers may find the resolution too tidy given the complexity of the issues presented.

**Questions for the room:**  
- Does the ending feel earned, or does it undermine the complexities introduced earlier?  
- How effectively does Fenichel balance the themes of honor and desire?  
- In what ways do the character arcs reflect or challenge modern perceptions of romance?  
- How does the dual POV enrich or complicate the story's conflict?  
- Are the societal critiques presented relevant in today's context?
