## Hook & thesis
In Chelsea Ichaso's gripping dark-thriller, **The Summer She Went Missing**, the narrative hinges on a community's collective silence surrounding the mysterious disappearance of a teenager, Audrey. The true engine of the story lies in exploring the complexities of guilt and complicity amidst a backdrop of teenage innocence corrupted by violence. **Verdict:** Ichaso crafts a compelling study of human nature, though its reliance on genre tropes occasionally undermines its emotional weight.

## The contextual pivot
Positioned within the dark-thriller genre, Ichaso's work resonates with other narratives centered on missing persons, echoing themes found in novels like *Gone Girl* by Gillian Flynn or *The Lovely Bones* by Alice Sebold. Within the author's oeuvre, this book introduces a more youthful perspective, targeting a young adult audience while also appealing to mature readers through its exploration of darker themes. The writing adeptly navigates the line between niche tropes—such as teen angst and societal scrutiny—and broader themes of accountability, making it accessible to a wider readership.

## Deep-dive critique
Ichaso's structure employs a non-linear timeline that weaves between the present investigation and flashbacks of the days leading up to Audrey's disappearance. This choice creates tension, drawing the reader into the characters' tangled emotions and motivations. The third-person limited perspective allows a closer connection to the protagonist while maintaining an air of mystery around the other characters, whose secrets gradually unfold. The prose is engaging, though it occasionally falters with overly melodramatic dialogue that detracts from the authenticity of the characters’ voices. 

Character development shines through the portrayal of the protagonist, whose internal conflict and moral dilemmas feel relatable, even if some secondary characters remain underexplored. Ichaso’s world-building, while effective in establishing a summer backdrop that contrasts with the story's darker elements, could benefit from deeper atmospheric detail to enhance immersion. The ending feels somewhat rushed, leaving questions about the resolution of key character arcs that could have been explored more thoroughly, thus impacting its emotional resonance.

## Discussion launchpad
The novel raises significant questions about accountability and the nuances of friendship under duress, polarizing opinions on whether the characters are relatable, sympathetic, or simply flawed. 

**Questions for the room:**  
- How does the exploration of guilt and complicity shape your understanding of the characters?  
- Do you believe the ending satisfactorily resolves the narrative tension, or does it leave too many loose ends?  
- In what ways does the book challenge or conform to typical tropes of the dark-thriller genre?  
- How effectively does the setting contribute to the overall tone and themes of the story?  
- What are the implications of the community's silence regarding Audrey's disappearance, and how does it reflect societal attitudes towards violence?
