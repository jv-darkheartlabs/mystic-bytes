## Hook & thesis
In *The Summer She Went Missing*, Chelsea Ichaso deftly explores the murky waters of secrecy and betrayal, crafting a narrative steeped in tension and emotional complexity. **Verdict:** This novel transforms a standard missing-person trope into a haunting meditation on truth, guilt, and the shadows of adolescence.

## The contextual pivot
Situated firmly within the dark-thriller genre, Ichaso's work resonates with echoes of contemporaries like Courtney Summers and Gillian Flynn, who also delve into the darkness lurking beneath seemingly innocent facades. This narrative uniquely captures the tumultuous summer of youth, where friendships blur lines of loyalty and safety, appealing not only to existing fans of YA thrillers but also inviting general readers to confront the unsettling truths of human relationships. Ichaso’s ability to bridge niche tropes—such as the unreliable narrator and the claustrophobic small-town setting—draws in an audience eager for psychological depth alongside the suspense of a missing person mystery.

## Deep-dive critique
Ichaso’s narrative structure is a labyrinthine interplay of perspectives that oscillates between the haunting past and the desperate present, a technique that magnifies the emotional weight of its central conflict. The pacing, while initially languid, mirrors the slow unraveling of the characters' facades, allowing tension to simmer as layers of deceit are peeled back. The choice of a fragmented timeline serves to underscore the protagonist's disorientation and the community's complicity in the silence surrounding Audrey's disappearance.

Characterization shines in Ichaso's portrayal of complex, flawed individuals whose desires and motivations often clash in chaotic ways. The dialogue crackles with an authenticity that captures the rawness of teenage emotions, yet it is the internal monologues that reveal profound insights into guilt and longing. However, while the emotional stakes are high, certain plot developments can feel predictable, diluting the impact of revelations that should be more shocking. The resolution, while satisfying in its thematic conclusions about truth and consequence, leaves some narrative threads underexplored, raising questions about the completeness of character arcs.

## Discussion launchpad
The novel’s exploration of truth and the consequences of silence invites heated debate, particularly around the ethical implications of the characters' choices and the societal pressures they face. Where does the line blur between protection and complicity? What does it mean to be an unreliable narrator in one’s own life? Moreover, the book's conclusion challenges readers to confront their own biases about accountability and justice.

**Questions for the room:**
- How does Ichaso's use of multiple perspectives affect your empathy for the characters?
- In what ways does the setting enhance or detract from the overall tension?
- Do you find the ending to be a resolution or an evasion of deeper truths?
- What role does the theme of friendship play in shaping the characters' decisions?
- How does the novel challenge or reinforce stereotypes about youth in crisis?
