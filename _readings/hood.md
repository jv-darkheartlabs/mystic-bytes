## Hook & thesis
In Stephen R. Lawhead's *Hood*, the author delves into the primal dance of power, loyalty, and desire amidst the backdrop of a reimagined Robin Hood legend, revealing that the heart's true rebellion is against the chains of societal expectation. **Verdict:** This novel masterfully entwines the dark allure of its characters' motivations with the intricate tapestry of its fantasy world, making for a compelling exploration of what it means to be a hero in a morally complex landscape.

## The contextual pivot
*Hood* navigates the rich terrain of fantasy-romance, standing alongside the likes of authors such as Robin McKinley and Juliet Marillier, who also weave romance and adventure into fantastical retellings. Lawhead’s work sits comfortably within the subgenre's embrace of both myth and romance, yet it pushes against the boundaries of traditional storytelling by imbuing its characters with a raw emotional intensity that may challenge genre norms. The invisible audience here spans both dedicated fantasy enthusiasts and those drawn to romance's compelling emotional arcs, as Lawhead bridges niche storytelling tropes with universal themes of love and loyalty.

## Deep-dive critique
The narrative structure of *Hood* serves to enhance its central themes, as Lawhead expertly employs a dual timeline, interspersing past and present to create tension and a sense of inevitability. The pacing, while at times languorous, allows for deep character exploration; particularly the protagonist, who grapples with his identity and the burden of his choices. The prose is lush and immersive, effectively conjuring the verdant landscapes of medieval England while simultaneously delving into the darker shades of human desire and conflict. 

Yet, while the character arcs are richly drawn, they occasionally veer into archetypal territory, which may pull some readers out of the emotional engagement. The dialogue, evocative and often lyrical, shines when it captures the characters’ hidden longings, although it falters in moments that feel overly expository. The world-building, infused with historical nuance, is generally compelling, yet some scenes suffer from a lack of clarity, leaving readers grappling with the relationships and stakes at play. Ultimately, the ending, while satisfying, feels rushed, leaving lingering threads that could have been deftly woven together.

## Discussion launchpad
The juxtaposition of loyalty versus personal desire is a rich vein for exploration, particularly how the characters navigate their relationships within the constraints of their societal roles. What does it mean to be a hero when your desires conflict with your responsibilities?  

**Questions for the room:**  
- How do you interpret the balance between duty and desire in the characters’ motivations?  
- In what ways does the dual timeline enhance or detract from the emotional impact of the story?  
- Are the characters’ arcs sufficiently resolved, or do they leave too much to be desired?  
- How does Lawhead’s treatment of fantasy violence reflect on the emotional stakes of the narrative?  
- Does the lush prose elevate the romantic and gothic elements, or does it risk overshadowing the narrative drive?
