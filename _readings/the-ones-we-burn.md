## Hook & thesis
In *The Ones We Burn*, Rebecca Mix intricately weaves a tale that explores the destructive power of desire and the moral complexities of vengeance. **Verdict:** This novel captivates with its potent blend of fantasy and romance, leaving readers questioning the fine line between love and obsession.

## The contextual pivot
Set against a backdrop of witch hunts and societal persecution, Mix's work operates within the fantasy-romance genre, positioning itself alongside authors like Sarah J. Maas and Holly Black, who also delve into dark themes of love intertwined with supernatural elements. However, Mix's unique voice and heavy thematic weight distinguish her narrative, allowing it to resonate with both dedicated genre fans and those drawn to deeper explorations of character and morality. The book engages readers who crave not only enchantment but also a reflection on the darker sides of desire and power dynamics.

## Deep-dive critique
The structure of *The Ones We Burn* deftly utilizes dual perspectives, allowing for a rich examination of the protagonists’ conflicting motivations while enhancing the narrative's tension. This duality serves to heighten the stakes as the characters navigate their complicated relationship amidst war and persecution. Mix’s prose is both lyrical and sharp, vividly depicting a world where fire symbolizes both destruction and passion. The dialogue is intricately crafted, revealing layers of longing and resentment that elevate the romantic tension without relying on overtly explicit descriptions. However, at times, the pacing falters in the middle sections, where moments of reflection meander, potentially losing readers yearning for momentum. Despite this, the climax is a masterclass in emotional payoff, where the characters confront the consequences of their choices in a fiery denouement that feels both inevitable and earned. Themes of obsession and sacrifice are woven throughout, confronting the reader with unsettling truths about love and loyalty in times of turmoil.

## Discussion launchpad
Mix's exploration of obsession blurs the boundaries of love and manipulation, igniting debates on moral ambiguity within relationships. Are the protagonists’ actions justified in the name of love, or do they cross an irredeemable line? 

**Questions for the room:**  
- How does the dual perspective shape your understanding of obsession versus love?  
- What are the implications of the portrayal of witch persecution in the narrative?  
- In what ways does the setting enhance the romantic tension between the characters?  
- Can the ending be viewed as a triumph or a tragedy? Why?  
- How does Mix's work challenge or conform to typical tropes in fantasy-romance?
