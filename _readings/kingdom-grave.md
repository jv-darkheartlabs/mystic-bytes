## Hook & thesis
In Shantel Tessier's *Kingdom Grave*, the interplay of obsession and desire propels the narrative toward a haunting climax that interrogates the nature of love entwined with darkness. **Verdict:** This novel exemplifies how the seductive allure of power can warp the most intimate of human connections into something perilously intoxicating.

## The contextual pivot
*Kingdom Grave* sits firmly within the dark billionaire subgenre, a realm where wealth and power often serve as both a shield and a prison. Tessier's work, laden with unrepentant desires and psychological complexity, aligns with authors like Pepper Winters and Rina Kent, who explore the depths of flawed relationships shaded by moral ambiguity. The book appeals to readers who thrive on the tension of forbidden love and perilous stakes, successfully bridging niche tropes with a broader audience drawn to tales of intense emotional conflict and the darker sides of romance.

## Deep-dive critique
Tessier's craft excels in her ability to dissect the psychological landscapes of her characters, particularly through the dual POV structure that allows readers to witness the tumultuous inner workings of both protagonists. The pacing, though occasionally uneven, builds a palpable tension that mirrors the characters' spiraling obsessions. For instance, the use of flashbacks reveals the traumas that fuel their desires, creating a layered narrative that invites empathy even as it unsettles. 

Dialogue is sharp and often laced with an undercurrent of menace, successfully establishing the stakes and the characters' motivations. Tessier's prose oscillates between lyrical and raw, effectively capturing the chaotic emotions of love intertwined with violence. However, the novel's darker themes—such as self-harm ideation and violence—may alienate some readers who are unprepared for the emotional weight; these elements are integral to the characters' arcs and the narrative's impact, yet their portrayal walks a fine line that could easily tip into sensationalism. The conclusion, while dramatic, feels earned as it encapsulates the duality of desire and destruction, leaving readers contemplating the cost of love in a world where darkness reigns.

## Discussion launchpad
The tension between love and obsession in *Kingdom Grave* prompts questions about morality, agency, and the nature of desire. How far can one go in the name of love before it becomes something else entirely? 

**Questions for the room:**  
- Do you think the portrayal of obsession as a form of love romanticizes unhealthy relationships?  
- How does Tessier's use of dual POV enhance or detract from the emotional impact of the story?  
- Are the content warnings effective in setting reader expectations, or do they hinder engagement with the themes?  
- In what ways does the ending challenge traditional notions of romantic fulfillment?  
- How do the characters' traumas inform your understanding of their actions, and do you find this sympathetic or problematic?
