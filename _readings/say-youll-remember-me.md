## Hook & thesis
In "Say You'll Remember Me," Abby Jimenez crafts a poignant exploration of love intertwined with the gravity of loss, set against the backdrop of a road trip that serves both as a journey and a reckoning. **Verdict:** This novel adeptly balances the sweetness of forbidden love with the harsh realities of life, creating a narrative that is as haunting as it is heartwarming.

## The contextual pivot
Positioned within the forbidden-love genre, Jimenez’s work resonates with readers familiar with the tension found in narratives like Colleen Hoover’s novels or Christina Lauren’s romantic escapades. However, it diverges significantly by delving deeper into themes of mortality and the shadows cast by terminal illness, nudging it closer to the territory of literary fiction. This book appeals to both niche audiences who revel in the emotional tumult of dark romance and general readers seeking relatable human experiences, making it a bridge between the two.

## Deep-dive critique
Jimenez's structure employs a dual perspective, allowing readers to see the intricacies of both protagonists' inner worlds, which amplifies the emotional stakes. The pacing is deliberate; moments of introspection are interspersed with scenes of tension, creating a rhythm that mirrors the characters' fluctuating hopes and fears. The language is rich yet accessible, intertwining lyrical passages with stark realities to reinforce the emotional depth — the juxtaposition of light and shadow is particularly poignant. 

Characters are well-drawn, with flaws and desires that pulse with authenticity; their dialogues crackle with chemistry, while also revealing vulnerabilities that enhance their relatability. However, some may find the reliance on certain genre tropes — such as the classic road trip motif — occasionally veers towards predictability, potentially undermining the novel's otherwise innovative take on grief and love. The ending, while satisfying, leaves lingering questions about the permanence of memory and the weight of love amidst loss, which can feel both earned and somewhat unresolved, inviting contemplation rather than closure.

## Discussion launchpad
The interplay of love and loss in "Say You'll Remember Me" offers rich avenues for discussion, particularly regarding the boundaries of love when faced with existential dilemmas.

**Questions for the room:**
- How effectively do you think the road trip serves as a metaphor for the characters' emotional journeys?
- Do you feel the balance between romantic tension and the heavier themes of terminal illness is well-executed, or does it detract from the love story?
- What are the implications of the ending? Does it resonate with the themes of remembrance and loss, or does it leave too much unresolved?
- How do you interpret Jimenez’s portrayal of forbidden love in the context of the characters’ challenges?
- What craft choices stood out to you as either strengths or weaknesses in the narrative?
