## Hook & thesis
*Gallant* by V.E. Schwab is a haunting exploration of memory, familial ties, and the shadows that cling to us. **Verdict:** Schwab masterfully uses the gothic form to weave a tale that interrogates the nature of belonging and the ghosts that shape us.

## The contextual pivot
Nestled within the gothic-horror-romance genre, *Gallant* stands as a testament to Schwab's prowess in creating richly layered narratives. Like Shirley Jackson's *We Have Always Lived in the Castle*, it navigates the treacherous waters of familial loyalty and the spectral echoes of the past. The novel appeals to an audience already familiar with the darker shades of fantasy, while seamlessly bridging niche tropes with broader themes of identity and acceptance. It calls to readers who find beauty in the grotesque, inviting them to confront their own shadows alongside the characters.

## Deep-dive critique
The structure of *Gallant* is compelling, operating on a dual-POV that juxtaposes the living with the lingering memories of the deceased, emphasizing the central theme of how past traumas reverberate through time. Schwab's pacing is deliberate yet engaging, allowing tension to build as the reader unravels the layered mysteries of the mansion and its inhabitants. The prose is imbued with a lyrical quality, effectively evoking the eerie atmosphere that permeates the story; Schwab's choice of descriptive language encapsulates the beauty and horror of the setting, making the mansion itself a character in its own right.

Characterization shines through the nuanced portrayals of the protagonist and the spectral figures that inhabit her world. The dialogue is both poignant and haunting, revealing the intricate dynamics at play within the family as well as the isolation felt by the protagonist. However, while the ending is thematically resonant, it may feel abrupt to some, leaving questions that linger like the shadows in the mansion. This choice, intentional or not, invites interpretation but could also frustrate those seeking closure.

## Discussion launchpad
This novel presents fertile ground for debate, particularly around the themes of isolation versus belonging and the spectral nature of memory. Readers may grapple with interpretations of the ending—was it a liberation or a deeper entrapment? The exploration of familial ties raises questions about loyalty and toxicity, challenging the notion of unconditional love.

**Questions for the room:**  
- How does Schwab's portrayal of the mansion influence your understanding of the characters' psyches?  
- In what ways does *Gallant* confront or reinforce traditional gothic tropes?  
- What does the ending suggest about the nature of memory and identity?  
- How do you interpret the relationship between the living and the dead in the narrative?  
- Are the themes of isolation and belonging ultimately resolved, or do they remain ambiguous?
