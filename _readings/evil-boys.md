## Hook & thesis
In *Evil Boys*, Clarissa Wild weaves a tale that dances on the razor's edge of desire and danger, where the allure of toxic relationships is both intoxicating and devastating. **Verdict:** This novel captivates through its exploration of the tangled psychology behind bullying and desire, making it a haunting reflection on the darker facets of romance.

## The contextual pivot
Positioned within the bully-romance subgenre, *Evil Boys* stands alongside notable titles like Penelope Douglas's *Fall Away* series, yet Wild’s narrative dives deeper into the gothic elements of masquerade and hidden identities. The author’s prose straddles the line between alluring and unsettling, appealing to a niche audience that revels in the complexities of dark romance while inviting general readers to explore themes of power, secrecy, and the volatile nature of adolescent love. Wild's previous works have similarly examined the tangled web of desire and danger, making this novel a fitting extension of her oeuvre.

## Deep-dive critique
Wild's deft craftsmanship shines through in her use of dual perspectives, allowing the reader to oscillate between the protagonists’ contrasting inner worlds. This structure amplifies the tension, as we witness how desire can morph into obsession, mirroring the volatile dynamics of high school cliques. The pacing, particularly in the first half, is deliberate, drawing readers into an intoxicating spiral of emotional manipulation that keeps the stakes high. However, the latter part of the book risks losing momentum as the plot leans into predictability, which may leave some readers craving a more surprising culmination. 

Characterization is another area where Wild excels; the protagonists are layered and relatable, ensnared in their own toxic cycles. Dialogues are sharp and evocative, often laced with an undercurrent of threat that emphasizes the push-pull nature of their interactions. However, the portrayal of secondary characters occasionally veers into cliché, which somewhat undermines the novel's exploration of complex relationships. The ending, while dramatic, feels somewhat rushed, lacking the emotional catharsis that would have elevated the characters' arcs to a more satisfying conclusion.

## Discussion launchpad
The juxtaposition of love and toxicity in *Evil Boys* invites a multitude of interpretations and raises polarizing questions about the nature of desire. How much of our understanding of romance is shaped by the darker aspects of human interaction? 

**Questions for the room:**  
- How do the themes of bullying and desire reflect societal attitudes towards toxic relationships?  
- In what ways does the dual perspective enhance or detract from the narrative's emotional impact?  
- Do you think the portrayal of secondary characters weakens or strengthens the main narrative?  
- How does the ending shape your perception of the protagonists' journeys?  
- What are the implications of romanticizing toxic relationships in literature?
