## Hook & thesis
Once You're Mine by Morgan Bridges delves deep into the murky waters of obsession and possessiveness, unraveling the psyche of its characters in a compelling exploration of desire gone awry. **Verdict:** This book is a riveting examination of how love can curdle into something dark and destructive, forcing readers to confront the thin line between passion and peril.

## The contextual pivot
Nestled firmly within the stalker-obsession subgenre, this novel stands out for its psychological depth and raw emotional landscape. Bridges, whose previous works often flirt with themes of darkness and desire, crafts a narrative that feels both niche and universally relatable, bridging the gap between dedicated genre fans and a broader audience intrigued by the human condition. The book's exploration of obsession aligns it with works like *You* by Caroline Kepnes and *Rebecca* by Daphne du Maurier, drawing on familiar tropes while infusing them with a fresh intensity.

## Deep-dive critique
Crafted with taut prose and a relentless pace, Once You're Mine thrives on its structure, shifting perspectives that heighten the tension and allow readers to experience the characters' psychological turmoil directly. The dual points of view create an unsettling intimacy, inviting readers to see the world through both the obsessive lens of the protagonist and his target — a decision that amplifies the stakes and the emotional dissonance. 

Bridges’ use of vivid imagery, particularly the recurring motifs of moths and skull imagery, underscores the themes of attraction to danger and the fragility of life. The prose is rich yet economical, a quality that draws readers into the characters' twisted motivations without overwhelming them with excess. 

However, the book occasionally falters in character development; certain secondary characters feel underexplored, leaving gaps in the narrative that could have strengthened the emotional stakes. The climax, while thrilling, may leave some readers questioning if the resolution truly pays off the tension that has been meticulously built throughout. Yet, the ambiguity of the ending serves a purpose, evoking lingering unease that resonates well beyond the final page.

## Discussion launchpad
The tension between devotion and destruction is palpable in this narrative, raising critical questions about the nature of love and obsession. This book will undoubtedly prompt discussions about the ethics of desire and the psychology of possessiveness. 

**Questions for the room:**  
- How do Bridges' thematic choices reflect or challenge societal views on love and obsession?  
- In what ways does the dual perspective enhance or detract from the reader's experience?  
- Can we separate the allure of the characters from their morally ambiguous actions, or do they become indistinguishable?  
- How does the imagery of moths and skulls function within the narrative — what do they symbolize for you?  
- Does the ending provide closure, or does it invite further contemplation on the nature of obsession?
