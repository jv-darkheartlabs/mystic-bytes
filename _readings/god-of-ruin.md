## Hook & thesis
Rina Kent's **God of Ruin** is an audacious exploration of power dynamics and emotional destruction in a world where gods walk among mortals. **Verdict:** This book intricately weaves torment and desire, crafting a narrative that lays bare the seductive allure of ruin.

## The contextual pivot
In the realm of bully romance, where emotional stakes are often overshadowed by physical conflict, **God of Ruin** stands as a compelling entry that melds dark themes with a rich supernatural backdrop. Kent's work occupies a space that bridges gritty college life with mythological grandeur, appealing to readers who relish the interplay of dominance and vulnerability. Influenced by authors like Penelope Douglas and the visceral intensity found in works such as *Cruel Prince*, Kent navigates familiar tropes while injecting them with a sharp, contemporary edge. The invisible audience likely includes those who embrace the depths of emotional turmoil wrapped in a romantic framework, but the book's broader appeal lies in its ability to elevate niche motifs for a general readership.

## Deep-dive critique
The structure of **God of Ruin** is a calculated dance between dual perspectives, offering a nuanced view of both protagonists as they grapple with their inner demons. This alternating POV maintains a tight grip on tension, allowing readers to witness the transformation of antagonism into reluctant desire. Kent’s prose is laden with metaphor and vivid imagery, enhancing the gothic undertones and elevating the stakes at every turn. Yet, while the dialogue crackles with sharpness and wit, some exchanges risk clashing with the otherwise dark ambiance, creating moments where humor feels jarring rather than organic.

The pacing, while initially sluggish as it establishes character backstories in a college setting, ultimately crescendos into a gripping climax that feels both inevitable and shocking. The thematic exploration of legacy—how past traumas shape present identities—resonates deeply, particularly in the context of the ‘godly’ elements entangled within the characters’ lives. However, the ending, while dramatic, may leave some readers grappling with the resolution's ambiguity; it asks whether true redemption is even possible when ruin has been the central theme.

## Discussion launchpad
This novel provokes a multitude of reactions, especially regarding its portrayal of toxic relationships and the glorification of emotional torment. What remains unspoken is whether the characters’ journeys toward ruin are authentic or simply a means to fulfill genre expectations. The line between love and destruction blurs, leaving readers to ponder the moral implications of such dynamics.

**Questions for the room:**  
- How do we reconcile the romanticization of toxic relationships in dark romance?  
- Is the ending of **God of Ruin** a triumph of character growth or a perpetuation of cycles of trauma?  
- In what ways does the dual POV enhance or detract from the emotional stakes?  
- Does the supernatural element elevate the narrative or distract from the core human conflict?  
- How effectively does Kent challenge or conform to genre conventions surrounding bully romance?
