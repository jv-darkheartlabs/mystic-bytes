## Hook & thesis
*Most of All You* by Mia Sheridan deftly navigates the treacherous waters of forbidden love, illuminating the emotional wreckage of its characters with both tenderness and intensity. **Verdict:** This is a haunting exploration of love's ability to mend or shatter, where desire and pain coexist in a dance as old as time.

## The contextual pivot
Within the dark romance genre, Mia Sheridan's work often plunges into the depths of emotional trauma and healing — a signature motif that resonates throughout her body of work. *Most of All You* finds its niche among narratives that intertwine longing and forbidden connections, echoing the works of authors like Colleen Hoover and Pepper Winters. This story, while steeped in dark themes, manages to bridge the gap between niche tropes and a broader audience, making it accessible to readers who may typically shy away from the more visceral aspects of romance. Sheridan crafts her narrative with a keen understanding of her audience’s need for both emotional catharsis and palpable tension.

## Deep-dive critique
Sheridan's prose is both lyrical and visceral, engaging the reader through a carefully structured dual POV that enhances the exploration of desire and the consequences of choices. The pacing oscillates between moments of breathless yearning and reflective pauses, allowing the reader to savor the weight of each emotional revelation. The timeline, unfolding in a non-linear fashion, adds layers of complexity, unveiling the characters' backstories as a means of deepening the present conflict. This technique effectively serves the central thesis of healing through love, as past traumas bleed into the present, shaping the characters’ interactions.

However, while the emotional depth is commendable, occasional moments of dialogue feel stilted, pulling the reader out of the immersive experience. The characters' motivations are compelling, yet some secondary characters lack dimension, serving primarily as foils to the protagonists rather than fully realized individuals. Still, the ending emerges as a potent affirmation of resilience and growth, earned through the struggles faced throughout the narrative.

## Discussion launchpad
The intricate dance of love and pain in *Most of All You* invites a wealth of discussion, particularly around the moral complexities inherent in forbidden love. What does it truly mean to love someone when the stakes are so high? How do we reconcile the characters' desires with the societal and personal repercussions they face? 

**Questions for the room:**  
- Do you believe that love can truly heal past wounds, or does it often perpetuate new ones?  
- How does the dual POV affect your emotional investment in the characters?  
- Are the moments of tension and desire sufficient to outweigh the mechanical flaws in dialogue and character development?  
- In what ways does the narrative challenge or reinforce traditional ideas of love?  
- What role does the setting play in amplifying the emotional stakes of the story?
