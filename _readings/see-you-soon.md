## Hook & thesis
In "See You Soon," Lexie Axelson crafts a haunting narrative that intertwines the haunting specters of grief and obsession within the gritty world of military romance. **Verdict:** Axelson’s exploration of love that transcends death is both provocative and unsettling, challenging the boundaries of desire and mortality.

## The contextual pivot
This novel finds its niche within the dark military romance subgenre, merging elements of the supernatural with profound emotional undertones. Axelson’s work resonates with fans of authors like Tillie Cole and Laura Thalassa, who similarly navigate the murky waters of desire amid loss and violence. It speaks to an audience that craves both the thrill of military life and the complexities of love entangled with death, bridging niche tropes to engage a broader readership that enjoys gothic and paranormal themes.

## Deep-dive critique
Axelson's narrative structure cleverly oscillates between present-day military settings and spectral encounters, allowing the protagonist’s grief to manifest physically and psychologically. The pacing, while occasionally meandering, serves to heighten the emotional stakes; moments of tension are drawn out, echoing the protagonist’s internal battle against the impending sense of loss and the allure of the supernatural. The point of view, primarily anchored in the protagonist's mind, invites readers into a deeply personal experience of obsession, yet at times creates a claustrophobic effect that may alienate some. 

The prose is laden with vivid imagery that evokes both the stark realities of military life and the ethereal quality of the spectral realm. Axelson's dialogue serves as a double-edged sword; while it occasionally falters into cliché, it often resonates with raw authenticity, especially in moments of grief and longing. The thematic exploration of death as both an end and a beginning is particularly poignant, though the resolution can feel rushed, leaving some questions around the characters' arcs inadequately addressed. The ending, while intense, may not satisfy all readers, as it challenges conventional resolutions in romance narratives.

## Discussion launchpad
The novel’s exploration of the intersection between love, death, and obsession raises significant questions and polarities that are ripe for discussion. How does Axelson balance the romanticization of trauma with the realities of military life? 

**Questions for the room:**  
- Does the supernatural element enhance or detract from the story’s emotional weight?  
- In what ways does the portrayal of military life align with or challenge traditional romance tropes?  
- How effectively does Axelson handle themes of grief and loss, and do they resonate authentically?  
- What implications does the novel's ending have for our understanding of love in the face of mortality?  
- How does the book's treatment of obsession reflect broader societal themes?
