## Hook & thesis
Georgia Hunter's *We Were the Lucky Ones* deftly navigates the harrowing landscape of family and survival against the stark backdrop of World War II. **Verdict:** This novel resonates deeply, revealing that the true luck lies not in escape, but in the bonds we forge against the relentless tide of history.

## The contextual pivot
Set within the dark corridors of historical fiction, Hunter's work stands as a poignant testament to familial love in times of unimaginable horror. This story sits comfortably alongside other prominent narratives such as Anthony Doerr's *All the Light We Cannot See* and Ruta Sepetys' *Salt to the Sea*, yet it carves its own space by intertwining personal trauma with collective memory. The invisible audience here is broad, yet discerning; while it speaks to fans of historical narratives, it also reaches those drawn to profound human connection, making the tale accessible beyond niche readers.

## Deep-dive critique
Hunter employs a multi-POV structure that enhances the emotional weight and urgency of her characters' journeys, allowing readers to traverse the fragmented world of war through the eyes of different family members. This choice amplifies the stakes, as each perspective adds layers of tension and desire for reunion amidst chaos. The pacing is masterful; Hunter deftly balances moments of harrowing despair with glimmers of hope, creating a rhythm that keeps readers invested. 

However, while the prose is often evocative, there are moments where it dances too close to sentimentality, risking overshadowing the rawness of the characters' experiences. Dialogue occasionally falters, feeling contrived in its effort to convey profound truths rather than allowing those truths to emerge organically through action. The world-building, particularly in its depiction of wartime Europe, is strikingly vivid yet sometimes lacks the depth needed to fully immerse the reader in the era’s horrors. Ultimately, the ending strives for resolution yet raises questions about the nature of survival and what it means to be 'lucky' when trauma lingers.

## Discussion launchpad
The narrative deftly engages with the complexities of survival, yet it leaves some questions lingering, particularly about the nature of luck and the ethics of memory. 

**Questions for the room:**  
- Does the multi-POV approach dilute the emotional impact, or does it enhance the understanding of collective trauma?  
- How effectively does Hunter navigate the balance between hope and despair?  
- In what ways does the concept of 'luck' evolve throughout the story, and does it resonate with wider historical contexts?  
- Are Hunter’s characters fully realized, or do they serve more as vehicles for thematic exploration?  
- What role does familial love play in the face of overwhelming adversity — does it uplift or ultimately weigh down the narrative?
