## Hook & thesis
Mia Sheridan's *Stinger* delves into the complexities of love entangled with danger, exploring how desire can flourish in the most perilous of circumstances. **Verdict:** This is a provocative exploration of forbidden love that challenges the boundaries of morality and consequence.

## The contextual pivot
Nestled within the forbidden-love subgenre, *Stinger* straddles the line between dark romance and psychological exploration, reminiscent of works by authors such as Anna Todd and K.A. Tucker. Sheridan’s narrative, steeped in urgency and tension, appeals to a niche audience craving intensity while simultaneously bridging to broader readerships through universal themes of heartbreak and redemption. The stakes, set against the vibrant yet shadowy backdrop of Las Vegas, provide a rich tapestry that both complements and contrasts the characters' inner turmoil.

## Deep-dive critique
Sheridan's skillful use of dual perspectives amplifies emotional weight, allowing readers to navigate the tumultuous inner worlds of her protagonists. The nonlinear timeline serves to heighten tension, creating a sense of inescapable fate that echoes the characters’ entangled lives. However, while the pacing is generally effective, there are moments where the narrative lingers excessively on past traumas, threatening to dilute the urgency that defines the book's core.

The prose is imbued with a lyrical quality that captures the rawness of desire against a backdrop of danger. Sheridan's dialogue, sharp and often charged, reveals much about the characters' motivations, yet at times, it slips into melodrama, which could alienate readers who crave authenticity. World-building, particularly the depiction of Las Vegas, oscillates between alluring and unsettling, mirroring the characters' complex emotional landscapes. The ending, while cathartic, risks feeling abrupt—leaving one pondering if the resolution is genuinely earned or merely an escape from the chaos.

## Discussion launchpad
*Stinger* presents a fertile ground for discussion, particularly regarding its handling of morality in love and the representation of trauma. 

- What moral ambiguities are presented, and do they enhance or detract from the narrative?
- How does the portrayal of violence and danger affect your perception of the romance within the story?
- In what ways does the dual perspective enrich or complicate the reader's understanding of the characters?
- Do you find the ending satisfying, or does it leave too many questions unanswered?

- What lines do we draw between romance and obsession within the story?

- Can love be justified in the face of violence, and what does the narrative suggest?
