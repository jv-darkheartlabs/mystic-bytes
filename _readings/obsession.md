## Hook & thesis
The novel **Obsession** by Shantel Tessier transcends its tropes of stalking and possessiveness to explore the darker recesses of human desire and morality. **Verdict:** This bold narrative invites readers into a labyrinth of obsession where the lines between love and madness are perilously blurred.

## The contextual pivot
Within the genre of dark romance, **Obsession** stands as a compelling examination of the complexities surrounding desire, power dynamics, and the often-unforgiving nature of human connection. Tessier, known for her exploration of the darker aspects of love, weaves a narrative that resonates with fans of contemporary romances that delve into the psychological. The book appeals to an audience comfortable with niche themes of obsession and sin, while still managing to bridge the gap to those who may find themselves drawn to the allure of forbidden desire.

## Deep-dive critique
The structure of **Obsession** is a careful orchestration of tension and release, with pacing that mirrors the frenetic and often chaotic nature of the protagonist's fixation. Tessier employs a dual perspective, offering insights into both the object of obsession and the obsessive character. This choice enhances the narrative's complexity, allowing readers to grapple with the motivations and justifications of both parties involved. The dialogue is charged, filled with subtext that hints at deeper vulnerabilities and desires, making each interaction fraught with tension.

However, while the prose is often evocative, there are moments where the melodrama threatens to overshadow the nuanced exploration of its themes. The portrayal of possessive behavior, ideally critiqued, occasionally veers into glorification, which could alienate readers seeking a more grounded examination of such dynamics. The ending, while climactic, raises questions on whether it provides closure or merely amplifies the cycle of obsession; it feels both earned and yet disconcertingly open-ended.

## Discussion launchpad
The provocative themes of **Obsession** are sure to ignite discussions on the nature of desire and the moral implications of obsession. How far can one go in the name of love before it becomes something monstrous?

**Questions for the room:**  
- Does the portrayal of obsession in this novel romanticize unhealthy relationships, or does it serve as a cautionary tale?  
- How effectively does Tessier navigate the themes of power imbalance in her characters' interactions?  
- In what ways does the dual perspective enhance or detract from the reader's understanding of obsession?  
- Is the ending of **Obsession** satisfying, or does it leave too many questions unanswered?  
- How does **Obsession** compare to other works in the dark romance genre regarding its treatment of psychological themes?
