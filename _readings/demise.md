## Hook & thesis
In *Demise*, Katelyn Taylor masterfully intertwines gothic horror and dark romance within an academic setting, exploring themes of obsession and the quest for identity. **Verdict:** This novel grips the reader with its haunting atmosphere and relentless tension, rendering it a memorable addition to the gothic romance canon.

## The contextual pivot
*Demise* occupies a distinct niche within the gothic-horror-romance genre, echoing the atmospheric tension of classic works by authors like Daphne du Maurier and Shirley Jackson while embedding itself in the contemporary landscape of dark academy narratives. Taylor, known for her ability to craft intricate worlds steeped in mystery, continues to draw parallels between desire and danger, inviting both dedicated fans of the genre and newcomers alike. The invisible audience is one that revels in the juxtaposition of intellect and dread, the scholarly pursuits that mask deeper, darker urges. 

## Deep-dive critique
Taylor's craft shines through her deft manipulation of pacing and structure — the first half of the novel slowly builds an ominous atmosphere, drawing the reader into the protagonist's world of antique books and academic rituals, while the latter half accelerates into a whirlwind of revelation and horror. The use of first-person POV immerses readers in the psyche of the protagonist, heightening both their isolation and their longing, which amplifies the tension throughout the narrative. The dialogue is sharp, often revealing unspoken truths and desires that lurk beneath the surface, effectively illuminating the characters’ inner conflicts. 

However, as the plot unfolds, there are moments where the reliance on familiar gothic tropes can feel a tad predictable. While the ending is bold and evocative, it risks alienating those who prefer more closure; instead, it leaves lingering questions that echo the novel’s themes of mortality and identity. This choice, while artistically resonant, may frustrate some readers who yearn for a more definitive resolution.

## Discussion launchpad
*Demise* provokes a rich tapestry of themes and emotions, creating polarities ripe for discussion. The interplay of life and death invites contemplation of how we shape identities in the face of impending demise, yet the characters' motivations often remain shrouded in ambiguity. 

**Questions for the room:**  
- How does the setting of an academic institution amplify the themes of obsession and identity?  
- In what ways does the novel challenge or conform to traditional gothic tropes?  
- Did the ending resonate with you, or did it feel incomplete? Why?  
- How does Taylor’s portrayal of desire influence the dynamics between characters?  
- What role does the motif of death play in shaping the protagonist's journey?
