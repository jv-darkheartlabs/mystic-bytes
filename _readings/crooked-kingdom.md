## Hook & thesis
*Crooked Kingdom* by Leigh Bardugo is an intoxicating exploration of loyalty's limits and the dark seductions of ambition. **Verdict:** This is a sumptuous tapestry of desire and betrayal, where the stakes are as high as the emotional toll.

## The contextual pivot
Nestled within the broader fantasy-romance genre, Bardugo’s work transcends mere tropes, crafting a compelling narrative that intertwines the allure of dark romance with the grit of heist and betrayal. This sequel to *Six of Crows* showcases Bardugo’s signature style, rich with morally ambiguous characters that challenge the reader's sympathies. The book caters not only to established fans of the Grishaverse but also beckons general readers with its nuanced exploration of trust and ambition, seamlessly bridging the niche of dark and romantic fantasy.

## Deep-dive critique
In *Crooked Kingdom*, the dual perspectives of Kaz Brekker and Inej Ghafa drive a riveting structure that juxtaposes their desires against a backdrop of chaos and loyalty. Bardugo's pacing is masterful, with tension escalating through a series of meticulously crafted plot twists that keep the reader on edge. The dialogue sparkles with wit and poignancy, capturing the characters' inner conflicts and desires without becoming overwrought. The world-building is both lush and treacherous, providing a setting that feels alive, resonating with the characters' own moral dilemmas. 

However, the intricate plot can at times feel overwhelming, occasionally sacrificing character depth for complexity. While the ending is both thrilling and satisfying, it raises questions about the permanence of change in a world that thrives on deception. The emotional arcs of the characters, especially Kaz and Inej, resonate deeply, showcasing the struggle between vulnerability and the protective walls built by trauma. 

## Discussion launchpad
The layers of desire and the cost of ambition create a fertile ground for debate. How does the interplay of loyalty and betrayal shape our understanding of love in this narrative? What does it mean to desire both power and connection when the two seem irreconcilable?

**Questions for the room:**  
- How do Kaz and Inej's relationships redefine traditional romance tropes?  
- In what ways does the setting of Ketterdam serve as a character itself, influencing the narrative?  
- What is the significance of choice versus fate in the characters' journeys?  
- How does Bardugo balance darkness with hope, and is it effective?  
- What unresolved tensions linger after the final page?
