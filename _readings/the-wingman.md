## Hook & thesis  
*The Wingman* by Stephanie Archer thrusts readers into a tempest of rivalry and unexpected intimacy, exploring the jagged edges of human connection. **Verdict:** Archer's narrative deftly intertwines the thrill of competition with the vulnerability of love, revealing that true desire often lurks beneath layers of animosity.

## The contextual pivot  
In the crowded realm of enemies-to-lovers tropes, *The Wingman* carves its niche through the lens of the competitive sports genre, particularly hockey. Archer's work resonates with fans of both romantic tension and adrenaline-fueled competition, reminiscent of titles like Elle Kennedy's *The Deal* and Tessa Bailey's *It Happened One Summer*. This book speaks to an audience hungry for emotional depth beneath the veneer of rivalry — an audience that thrives on the push and pull of attraction that simmers between adversaries. Archer's previous works suggest a mastery of blending romance with intricate character dynamics, positioning *The Wingman* as both a continuation and evolution of her thematic preoccupations.

## Deep-dive critique  
Archer's prose is sharp and evocative, capturing the frenzied emotions of both sport and romance. The pacing is particularly noteworthy; it accelerates in moments of tension, mirroring the heartbeat of a hockey game, while allowing for reflective pauses that amplify the central conflict. The dual perspectives enrich the narrative, providing insight into both protagonists' psyches and illuminating the fragility of their burgeoning connection amid the chaos of their rivalry. 

Dialogue serves as a vehicle for both character development and world-building, with witty banter layered with undercurrents of desire that heighten the stakes. However, the plot occasionally stumbles in its reliance on familiar tropes; certain character arcs could benefit from deeper exploration to fully capitalize on the emotional weight of their histories. The climax, while thrilling, feels somewhat rushed, leaving the resolution to skirt the edges of believability, yet the emotional payoff still resonates.

## Discussion launchpad  
The interplay between competition and intimacy in *The Wingman* raises questions about the nature of desire — can love truly blossom from enmity, or does it require a foundation of respect? The book's approach to character flaws presents another point of contention; do they enhance relatability or undermine the protagonists’ journey?

- How does the ending align with or diverge from typical enemies-to-lovers narratives?  
- Are there emotional risks taken in the story that resonate beyond the tropes?
