## Hook & thesis
In *Eyes Turned Skyward*, Rebecca Yarros crafts a haunting exploration of love and loss set against the backdrop of military conflict, where the true engine propelling the narrative is the struggle between duty and desire. **Verdict:** This novel masterfully intertwines the visceral weight of grief with the intoxicating pull of passion, yielding a compelling yet devastating romance.

## The contextual pivot
Situated firmly within the dark-military-romance genre, Yarros’ work stands out for its emotional depth and raw authenticity. Unlike typical romance narratives that may lean into escapism, this book confronts the brutal realities of war—its aftermath, the toll on relationships, and the shadow it casts over intimacy. Yarros occupies a unique niche in contemporary romance, one that bridges the gap between genre-specific tropes and broader themes of human resilience, appealing not only to devoted fans of military narratives but also to readers seeking profound emotional engagement.

## Deep-dive critique
Yarros employs a dual narrative structure that alternates between past and present, effectively building tension and deepening character development. This technique allows the reader to witness the protagonist’s evolution, revealing how trauma shapes her understanding of love and self-worth. The pacing, however, can falter; moments of intense emotional revelation sometimes yield to slower sections that risk losing momentum. 

Characterization is where Yarros truly excels. The protagonist embodies vulnerability and strength, her internal conflicts rendered with nuance, while the supporting cast provides a rich tapestry of perspectives on loyalty and sacrifice. Dialogue, often imbued with raw honesty, captures the complexities of relationships forged in the crucible of conflict. Yet, while the prose is lyrical, at times it tips into melodrama, particularly in scenes laden with emotional weight.

The thematic exploration of grief is poignant, underscoring how love can both heal and wound, a duality that resonates throughout the narrative. Yarros does not shy away from depicting the darker sides of love, encapsulated in the grief that lingers like a ghost, haunting the characters long after the battle has ceased. The ending, while cathartic, raises questions about closure and the potential for new beginnings in the face of enduring loss. It feels earned yet leaves the reader pondering the cost of healing.

## Discussion launchpad
The novel's exploration of love within the constraints of trauma and duty creates a fertile ground for discussion, particularly around the complexities of military relationships and the psychological impact of war.

**Questions for the room:**  
- How does Yarros balance the themes of love and grief? Does one overshadow the other?  
- In what ways does the military setting enhance or detract from the romantic elements?  
- Can the depiction of grief in romance be considered a form of healing, or does it risk romanticizing trauma?  
- What are the implications of the ending for the characters’ futures—does it suggest hope or further entrapment?  
- How do Yarros' characterizations challenge or conform to traditional military romance tropes?
