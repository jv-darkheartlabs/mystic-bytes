## Hook & thesis
*The Shadow Glass* by Josh Winning plunges readers into a world where desire intertwines seamlessly with dread, crafting a tapestry of gothic horror that unsettles as much as it entices. **Verdict:** This haunting narrative explores the destructive potency of obsession, revealing how love can morph into an insidious force that consumes and distorts.

## The contextual pivot
Nestled within the gothic-horror-romance genre, *The Shadow Glass* emerges as a fervent homage to the classics while boldly forging its own path. Winning channels the eerie atmospheres of authors like Shirley Jackson and Angela Carter, yet weaves a distinctively contemporary thread through themes of longing and personal demons. This novel not only caters to niche enthusiasts of dark romance but also beckons a broader audience, bridging the gap between the esoteric and the universally relatable struggles of desire and loss.

## Deep-dive critique
Winning's craftsmanship shines through his deliberate pacing and atmospheric world-building. The narrative unfolds through a well-curated lens, employing a dual perspective that deepens our understanding of the characters' internal conflicts. However, the pacing occasionally falters, particularly in the middle sections, where a more relentless momentum could have heightened the tension embedded in the protagonists' spiraling desires. The prose is rich and evocative, often drawing the reader into a sensory experience that is both lush and oppressive, mirroring the characters' emotional states. Dialogue is sharp, often laced with subtext that reveals the characters' hidden fears and desires, though at times, it can slip into melodrama that detracts from the overall impact. The climax, while satisfying, risks feeling abrupt, leaving some thematic threads tantalizingly unresolved, which may frustrate some readers but also invites further contemplation on the nature of obsession and love.

## Discussion launchpad
The interplay of desire and horror in Winning's narrative raises fascinating questions of morality, obsession, and the nature of love itself. The exploration of these themes creates polarizing interpretations that could ignite passionate discussions among readers.

**Questions for the room:**  
- Does the dual perspective enhance the narrative's complexity or dilute its emotional impact?  
- How effectively does the author balance gothic tropes with modern sensibilities?  
- In what ways does the resolution challenge or conform to traditional gothic endings?  
- How does the theme of obsession resonate with contemporary societal views on love and relationships?  
- What does the novel suggest about the boundary between devotion and destruction?
