## Hook & thesis
*When in Rome* by Sarah Adams is a tantalizing exploration of the complexities of love ignited in the embers of rivalry. **Verdict:** This book masterfully juxtaposes the sweetness of culinary romance with the bitterness of emotional conflict, creating a richly layered narrative that captivates and entices.

## The contextual pivot
Positioned within the enemies-to-lovers sub-genre of dark romance, *When in Rome* casts a delicate balance between playful banter and underlying tension. Adams crafts a world where culinary arts serve as both battleground and sanctuary, reminiscent of the works of Talia Hibbert and Emily Henry, albeit with a darker, more seductive undertone. The audience, likely familiar with romance tropes yet yearning for depth, will find a unique blend of humor and emotional stakes that bridges niche interests with broader appeal, inviting even the most reluctant readers to indulge in its charms.

## Deep-dive critique
Adams employs a dual perspective that grants readers intimate access to both protagonists' inner turmoil, fostering an intense connection with their struggles. The pacing is deftly handled; the initial clashes are swift and sharp, igniting a spark that simmers into a slow burn. This structure effectively maintains tension, allowing desire to build organically. Prose flourishes when depicting culinary scenes, showcasing a sensory-rich experience that enhances emotional depth — the crackle of pastries, the warmth of fresh bread, all symbolize the characters' evolving relationship. 

Characterization shines through their interactions — snappy dialogue laden with subtext reveals vulnerabilities and hidden desires, though at times, the banter risks veering into cliché. The world-building draws readers into a vibrant, bustling Rome, yet occasionally lacks the atmospheric weight that could further elevate the stakes, leaving moments feeling slightly superficial. The resolution serves as a fitting climax, where emotional catharsis is artfully balanced with romantic fulfillment, though some readers may find it too neatly tied up, yearning for a grittier ending that reflects the book's darker undertones.

## Discussion launchpad
The interplay of rivalry and romance raises compelling questions about the nature of desire and conflict. While the enemies-to-lovers trope is beloved, the way it is executed here invites scrutiny — does the humor dilute the emotional stakes? Moreover, the culinary backdrop is enchanting, yet does it serve the narrative or merely act as a whimsical element?

**Questions for the room:**  
- How do humor and tension coexist in relationships, and does this book balance them effectively?  
- In what ways does the culinary theme enhance or distract from the central conflict?  
- Is the resolution satisfying, or does it undermine the darker elements explored throughout?  
- How does the dual perspective impact your connection to the characters?  
- What elements of the enemies-to-lovers trope feel fresh or tired in this narrative?
